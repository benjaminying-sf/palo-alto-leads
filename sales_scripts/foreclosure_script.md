# Pre-Foreclosure (Notice of Default) Call Script

**Use this when contacting:** The property owner who received the NOD. You must reach the owner, not the trustee — the trustee is the lender's representative, not the seller.

**Why they're motivated:** The owner is 90+ days behind on their mortgage. They face losing everything in foreclosure with zero proceeds. Selling to you lets them walk away with equity (if any), preserves their credit relative to a full foreclosure, and ends the stress.

**Timing is critical:** From the NOD date, the owner typically has ~90–180 days before a Trustee Sale. The sooner you reach them, the more options they have.

**Finding the owner's phone:** The NOD names the borrower (owner). Use the Google search link in the report, or search Whitepages.com with their name + address.

---

## OPENER

> "Hi, is this [Owner Name]?"

*(If they ask who's calling)*
> "My name is [Your Name] — I'm a local real estate investor. I wanted to reach out about your property on [Street Name]. I'll be brief."

---

## WHEN THEY ANSWER

> "Hi [Name], my name is [Your Name] — I'm a real estate investor based here in Palo Alto.
>
> I want to be straightforward with you: I came across a public record showing that a Notice of Default was filed on your property at [Address], and I wanted to reach out personally before things go further.
>
> I completely understand this can be an incredibly stressful situation — I'm not here to judge or pressure you. I work with homeowners in exactly this situation and I buy properties for cash. If you'd like, I can move very quickly, pay off what's owed to the lender, and potentially put cash in your pocket at closing — depending on what's owed versus what the home is worth.
>
> Is that something you'd be open to talking about for just a few minutes?"

---

## IF THEY'RE INTERESTED

> "Great. Let me ask a few quick questions so I can give you a realistic picture:
>
> 1. Roughly how much is still owed on the mortgage?
> 2. Is there anything else against the property — second mortgage, HELOC, or liens?
> 3. What condition is the property in — has it been updated recently, or does it need work?
>
> *(Listen and take notes)*
>
> Based on what I'm hearing, I think there's a real possibility I can help. Here's what I'd like to do: I'll come see the property in person — that takes about 30 minutes — and within 24 hours I'll give you a written all-cash offer. No obligation at all. If the numbers work for you, we can close in as little as 10 days. If they don't, you haven't lost anything.
>
> Does that sound fair? When could we meet at the property?"

---

## IF THEY SAY "I'm handling it / working with the bank"

> "That's great, and I respect that. The bank's workout options — forbearance, loan modification — are worth exploring. The only thing I'd caution is that those processes can sometimes take longer than people expect, and the clock on the foreclosure keeps running.
>
> My offer is just another option to have in your back pocket. If the bank works out a deal for you, wonderful. If that falls through, I can still move fast. Would it be okay to just exchange contact info in case you want to reach out?"

---

## IF THEY SAY "I don't want to sell"

> "I completely understand and I respect that. It's your home. I just wanted to make sure you had all your options in front of you.
>
> One thing worth knowing: if the property does go to foreclosure auction, the lender typically gets what they're owed and you walk away with nothing — even if the home is worth significantly more. Selling now, even at a slight discount to market, often puts real money in your pocket that a foreclosure would eliminate.
>
> I'll leave it there. My number is [Phone] — please don't hesitate to call if anything changes. I can move fast when needed."

---

## IF THEY'RE AGGRESSIVE / DEFENSIVE

> "I understand — I know this isn't an easy call to receive and I appreciate your patience. I'll let you go. My name is [Your Name] and my number is [Phone]. If anything changes, I'm here and I can move quickly. Take care."

*(Don't argue, don't push. Angry homeowners sometimes call back weeks later when they're in a worse spot.)*

---

## OBJECTIONS

| Objection | Response |
|-----------|----------|
| "How did you get my number?" | "The Notice of Default is a public record filed with the county. I search those records to find homeowners I might be able to help." |
| "Are you a realtor?" | "No — I'm an investor, not a realtor. I buy the property myself with cash, so there's no commission taken from you, no listing, no open houses." |
| "Your offer will be too low." | "I understand that concern. Palo Alto values are strong, and I'm well aware of that. I don't lowball — I make fair offers based on the property's actual condition and what's owed. Let me see the property and you can decide if the number works." |
| "I need more time to think." | "Of course. I just want to make sure you know the NOD timeline — you likely have until [estimated date] before a Trustee Sale can happen. If you want to keep your options open, the sooner we talk numbers, the more flexibility you have." |

---

## CLOSING

> "Let me send you a quick text right now so you have my name and number. What's the best email to send you a one-page overview of how my process works?
>
> I'll follow up in a few days to see if you have any questions. Sound good?"

---

## NOTES

- **Best time to call:** Early morning (8–9am) or after work (5–6pm) — owners are often home
- **Empathy is everything:** These are real people in financial distress. Investors who lead with empathy get far more deals than those who push hard
- **Mail them too:** Send a handwritten or personally addressed letter to the property address — many owners respond better to mail than cold calls
- **Follow up cadence:** Day 1 (call), Day 4 (text), Day 10 (call again), Day 21 (letter)
- **Key advantage:** Speed + cash + no lender approval waiting period
